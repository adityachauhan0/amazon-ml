    Causal Inference and Learning Paradigms in Data Science¶

    1. Causal Questions in Data Science¶

    Causal questions form the foundation of most scientific inquiries.

    • Policy Decisions: If we adopt a treatment/policy, will it lead to healthier patients or higher revenue?
    • A/B Experiments: If we change the algorithm, will it improve performance?
    • Credit Attribution: Are people buying because of the recommendation algorithm, or would they have purchased anyway?

    Formal Representation¶

    Let \(T\) denote a treatment (policy, intervention), and \(Y\) denote the outcome.

    We seek: \(\(P(Y \mid do(T)) \neq P(Y \mid T)\)\)

    2. Causation vs Correlation¶

    • Example: Ice-cream sales are correlated with sunburn cases.
    • However, reducing ice-cream sales will not reduce sunburn. Both are driven by hot weather.

    Diagram¶

    graph LR
  HW(Hot Weather) --> IC(Ice Cream Sales)
  HW --> SB(Sunburn)
  IC -- Correlation --> SB

    3. Confounders¶

    Confounders are variables influencing both treatment and outcome.

    • Example: Studying the impact of exercise (X) on cholesterol (Y).
    • At first glance, exercise and cholesterol appear positively correlated.
    • But age is a confounder: older individuals exercise less and have higher cholesterol.

    Partitioned Analysis¶

    • Younger individuals: More exercise, lower cholesterol.
    • Older individuals: Less exercise, higher cholesterol.

    Thus: $$ Age \rightarrow (X, Y) $$

    Diagram¶

    graph TD
  A(Age) --> X(Exercise)
  A --> Y(Cholesterol)
  X --> Y

    4. Randomized Control Trials (RCTs)¶

    • The gold standard for causal inference.
    • Randomly assigns treatments, removing confounding.
    • Limitations: expensive, slow, and often infeasible.

    Mathematical Setup¶

    For treatment group \(T=1\) and control \(T=0\):

    \[ATE = E[Y \mid do(T=1)] - E[Y \mid do(T=0)]\]

    5. Selection Bias¶

    • In observational studies, treatments are selectively allocated.
    • This causes biased estimates of treatment effects.
    • Example: Simpson’s paradox — subgroup effects differ from aggregate effects.

    Example Table¶

    Age Group Treatment A Treatment B
    Young 87% 92%
    Older 69% 73%
    Overall 83% 78%
    \[ P(Y \mid T, Age) \neq P(Y \mid T) \]

    6. Potential Outcomes Framework¶

    Also known as the Neyman-Rubin Model.

    • Defines causal effect via counterfactuals.
    • Each unit has two potential outcomes: \(Y_i(1)\) (treated), \(Y_i(0)\) (control).
    • But we only observe one of them.

    Notation¶

    • \(T\): Treatment
    • \(Y\): Outcome
    • Causal effect for individual \(i\):
    \[ \tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \]

    Example Table¶

    \(i\) \(T\) \(Y\) \(Y(1)\) \(Y(0)\) \(Y(1)-Y(0)\)
    1 0 0 ? 0 ?
    2 1 1 1 ? ?
    3 1 0 0 ? ?

    7. Data Generative Process¶

    • A set of variables \(T\) affect both cause and effect (confounders).
    • Observed data: \((X_i, T_i, Y_i(T_i))\).

    Graphical Model¶

    graph LR
  X(Features $X_i$) --> T(Treatment $T_i$)
  X --> Y(Outcome $Y_i$)
  T --> Y

    8. Supervised Learning¶

    • Observed data: tuples \((X_i, T_i, Y_i)\) sampled i.i.d.
    • Goal: Learn function \(Y_i = f(X_i, T_i)\).
    • Assumes training and test distributions are the same.

    Key Properties¶

    • No counterfactual reasoning.
    • \(T_i\) not determined by \(X_i\).

    Diagram¶

    graph TD
  X(Features $X_i$) --> Y(Outcome $Y_i$)
  T(Treatment Feature $T_i$) --> Y

    9. Reinforcement Learning¶

    • Data: \((X_i, T_i, Y_i)\) where \(T_i\) are actions and \(Y_i\) are rewards.
    • Data not i.i.d., but correlated via a Markov Decision Process.
    • Goal: Maximize long-term return, not predict single \(Y_i\).

    Properties¶

    • May be model-based or model-free.
    • Sequential decisions matter.

    Diagram¶

    graph TD
  X(Context $X_i$) --> A(Action $T_i$)
  A --> Y(Outcome/Reward $Y_i$)
  X --> Y

    10. Examples¶

    • Supervised Learning: Forecasting temperature using historical data.
    • Causal Inference: Uplift modeling of consumer demand under price changes.
    • Reinforcement Learning: AlphaGo learning to win at Go.

    11. Treatment Effect Definitions¶

    Causal effect can be defined at multiple levels.

    Average Treatment Effect (ATE)¶

    \[ATE = E(Y(T=1)) - E(Y(T=0))\]

    Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE)¶

    \[CATE(X) = E(Y(T=1) \mid X) - E(Y(T=0) \mid X)\]

    Individual Treatment Effect (ITE)¶

    \[ITE_i = Y_i(T=1) - Y_i(T=0)\]

    Summary¶

    • Correlation vs Causation distinction is crucial.
    • Confounders distort naive associations.
    • RCTs solve confounding but are costly.
    • Selection bias leads to paradoxical inferences.
    • Potential Outcomes define causal effects rigorously.
    • Supervised vs Reinforcement Learning differ in assumptions.
    • Treatment Effects (ATE, CATE, ITE) quantify impact at different levels.

    Supervised Learning¶

    • Observed data in the form of tuples: \(\((X_i, T_i, Y_i)\)\) sampled i.i.d.
    • Objective: model \(Y_i = f(X_i, T_i)\)
    • Not a counterfactual problem — test distribution = training distribution.
    • \(T_i\) values are not determined by the rest of the features \(X_i\).

    Diagram:¶

    flowchart TD
    X[Features (X_i)] --> Y[Outcome (Y_i)]
    T[Treatment Feature (T_i)] --> Y

    Reinforcement Learning¶

    • Data in the form of tuples: \(\((X_i, T_i, Y_i)\)\).
    • \(T_i\) → actions, \(Y_i\) → rewards.
    • Data points are not i.i.d.; they are correlated with Markov structure.
    • Goal is not to predict \(Y_i\), but to maximize long-term return.

    Diagram:¶

    flowchart TD
    C[Context (X_i)] --> O[Outcome (Y_i)]
    A[Action (T_i)] --> O

    Examples¶

    • Supervised Learning: Forecasting (temperature, humidity prediction).
    • Causal Inference: Uplift modeling (consumer demand vs. price change).
    • Reinforcement Learning: AlphaGo (play moves to win the game).

    Treatment Effect¶

    Treatment effect is measured at multiple levels: 1. Population level (ATE): $$ E(Y(T=1)) - E(Y(T=0)) $$ 2. Subgroup level (CATE): $$ E(Y(T=1)|X) - E(Y(T=0)|X) $$ 3. Individual level (ITE): $$ Y_i(T=1) - Y_i(T=0) $$

    Doubly Robust Estimation¶

    • Uses regression models \(m_0(x)\) and \(m_1(x)\).
    • Consistent if at least one model is correct.
    • Unbiased if one is correct, low bias but high variance compared to IPW.

    Sub-Classification¶

    • Splits population into homogeneous subgroups.
    • Each subgroup equally likely to obtain treatment.
    • Data from subgroups behaves like RCT data.
    • Suitable for binary treatments with propensity score binning.

    Diagram:¶

    flowchart TD
    P[Population] --> S1[Subgroup 1]
    P --> S2[Subgroup 2]
    P --> S3[Subgroup 3]
    S1 --> T1[Treatment / Control Comparison]
    S2 --> T2
    S3 --> T3

    Balanced Representation Learning Methods¶

    • TARNET (Treatment Agnostic Representation Network).
    • BNN (Balancing Neural Networks).
    • Inspired by Domain Adaptation Models.

    TARNET¶

    • Defines Factual & Counterfactual Losses.
    • Factual loss: predict actual outcome.
    • Counterfactual loss: predict outcome for different treatment.
    • Challenge: Counterfactual loss cannot be directly observed.

    Integral Probability Metrics (IPM)¶

    • Metric to calculate distance between distributions.
    • Used in balancing representations.

    Diagram:¶

    flowchart LR
    P1[Distribution P(X|T=0)] --> D[IPM]
    P2[Distribution P(X|T=1)] --> D[IPM]
    D --> R[Representation Alignment]

    Estimating IPM¶

    • Estimated using Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD).
    • Efficient computation with kernels.
    • Compares within-group vs across-group similarities.

    Balancing Neural Network¶

    • Confounders represented by a layer in a neural net.
    • Matching performed by minimizing IPM of representations.
    • Factual predictions derived from balanced representation.

    Causal Forest¶

    • Extension of Random Forests.
    • RF: choose splits that reduce MSE.
    • CF: splits maximize treatment–outcome relationship.
    • Produces consistent estimates of treatment effect.

    Final Key Insights¶

    • Supervised vs RL vs Causal Inference: different objectives and data assumptions.
    • Treatment Effect Estimation: multiple approaches (ATE, CATE, ITE).
    • Advanced Techniques: DR estimation, sub-classification, balancing representations.
    • Representation Learning: reduces confounding bias by aligning distributions.
    • Causal Forests & Meta Learners: powerful for heterogeneous treatment effects.
